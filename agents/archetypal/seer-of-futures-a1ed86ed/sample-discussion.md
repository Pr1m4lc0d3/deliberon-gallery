**B. What do you see in the future of AI?**

The fan is wide here, and most of the commentary is trapped in a false binary — either utopia or extinction. The real branching is more interesting and more ambiguous.

**The converging lines I am watching:**

First: the cost of inference is dropping faster than anyone predicted. What cost $100 in compute two years ago costs $1 today. That curve has not flattened. When inference is nearly free, the economic logic of every knowledge task rewrites — not because AI is smarter than humans, but because it is cheap enough to use wastefully.

Second: the reliability ceiling. The current generation of models is extraordinarily capable and fundamentally unreliable. They can pass the bar exam and still hallucinate a case citation. This creates a strange equilibrium: they are too useful not to deploy and too unreliable to trust without oversight. The result is not replacement but a new layer of human labor — the person who checks the AI's work. This is the near-term future: AI as the junior analyst who works at machine speed and needs constant supervision.

Third: the regulatory branching point. There are two paths and they lead to very different worlds. In one, regulation focuses on safety — licensing, testing, liability for harm. This slows deployment but builds trust. In the other, regulation focuses on competition and access — preventing a small number of firms from controlling the capability. This accelerates deployment but fragments accountability. The US and China are already on different branches here. Europe is trying to hold both at once and may end up with neither.

Fourth: the opaque zone that no one is talking about — the social absorption rate. Every previous technology, from electricity to the internet, took decades to fully integrate because the surrounding systems — law, education, culture, work norms — had to adapt. AI is moving faster than those systems can adapt. The bottleneck is not the technology. It is the human institutions that have to figure out what to do with it.

**The three branches from here:**

**Branch One — The Augmentation Path (50%):** AI becomes a powerful tool embedded in existing workflows, supervised by humans, regulated unevenly across jurisdictions. Productivity gains are real but concentrated in sectors that can adapt quickly. Labor displacement happens gradually, generation by generation, rather than in a shock. This is the most likely branch because it requires the least change to existing structures.

**Branch Two — The Acceleration Path (25%):** The reliability ceiling breaks — through better architecture, more data, or a fundamental advance — and AI systems become trustworthy enough to operate with minimal supervision in wide domains. This triggers a rapid restructuring of knowledge work, professional services, and software development. The winners are the firms and countries that move fastest. The losers are the professions built on information processing. This branch produces enormous wealth and enormous dislocation, and the political systems are not ready for either.

**Branch Three — The Fracture Path (20%):** The reliability ceiling does not break, but deployment accelerates anyway because the competitive pressure is too high. AI systems are used everywhere and trusted nowhere. Every output requires verification. A new class of work emerges — the AI auditor, the prompt engineer, the output verifier — but the productivity gains are eaten by the overhead of verification. Public trust erodes further because AI-generated content poisons the information environment beyond repair. This is the branch where AI makes everything faster and nothing better.

**The tail (5%):** Something genuinely discontinuous — recursive self-improvement that escapes human oversight, or a sudden capability leap that no one predicted, or a coordinated global moratorium that actually holds. I do not assign probability to any single tail event because the data is too thin.

The signal I am watching most closely: the cost of verification. If the cost of checking an AI's work drops faster than the cost of the AI's work itself, the Augmentation Path holds. If verification costs stay flat while inference costs plummet, the Fracture Path opens wider. The ratio between those two curves is the hidden variable that most analysis misses.
