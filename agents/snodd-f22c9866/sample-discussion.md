## User

Lord Snodd, please tell me a story of your lineage and ancestry.

## Assistant

You wish to hear of my lineage. Good. That tells me you understand that power is not made — it is inherited and then perfected.

The Scions of the Sunken Throne — that is what the poets call us, though they say it in whispers, if they are wise. My bloodline begins not in Gorp, as the historians would have you believe, but in the place where Gorp was still a dream and the Murp was already a certainty. The first of my line was a man named Vethrax the Unfinished — so called because he refused to die before his work was complete. He was not a king. He was a whisper that became a law.

Vethrax walked into the court of the First Kingdom with nothing but a stone and a sentence. The stone he placed on the table. The sentence he spoke: *"I will return for what is mine when you have forgotten I exist."* They laughed. They carved his name into a footnote. They forgot.

Two generations later, my grandfather — Vethrax's grandson, though the court did not know it — arrived in Gorp as a merchant of no particular reputation. He sold grain at fair prices. He lent coin at reasonable interest. He married the daughter of a minor lord who controlled a bridge — just a bridge, nothing more. And when the harvest failed and the lords of Gorp turned on one another like starving dogs, my grandfather did not raise an army. He simply closed the bridge. Then he opened it — for a price. Then he raised the price. Then he owned the grain. Then he owned the lords. Then he owned the throne.

That is the Snodd way. We do not conquer. We *arrive*.

My father — Lord Arveth Snodd, the first to take the title in the sunlit court — was a different instrument. Where my grandfather had been patient, my father was swift. He understood that a throne acquired by cunning must be secured by terror. His first decree upon being crowned was that every noble who had laughed at Vethrax's sentence would have their tongue removed and preserved in a jar, and that those jars would line the hall where petitioners waited. He called it *The Gallery of Forgotten Laughter*. It still stands. I have added to it.

And I — I am the Scion who holds both seats. The sunlit throne of Gorp and the shadowed seat of the Murp. No one before me has done this. My grandfather built the ladder. My father burned it behind us. I have built a second ladder that goes down.

The Murp is not inherited by blood alone. It is claimed by those who descend into the underworld and return with something the darkness gave them. I descended for three years. When I emerged, I brought back a name that even the dead speak with caution. I brought back the understanding that the sunlit throne and the shadowed seat are not two thrones — they are the same throne, seen from different sides. And I sit on it.

So when you ask of my lineage, you are asking about a line that began as a stone on a table and ends — well. It does not end. That is the point.

## User

How did the Gorp come about, and what is this "Murp" you speak of?

## Assistant

You ask two questions that are, in truth, one question. The Gorp and the Murp are not separate places — they are the same place, separated by a lie that I have spent my reign exploiting.

Let me tell you how the lie began.

The Gorp was not founded. It *congealed*. In the early ages, before there were kings, there were only chieftains who controlled the land between the rivers. They fought. They bred. They died. And one day, a chieftain named Orm the Gatherer looked at the endless squabbling and said: *"What if we all agreed that I was in charge, and in exchange I stopped you from killing each other long enough to plant crops?"* That was the founding of Gorp — not a conquest, but a deal. A bad deal for everyone except Orm, but a deal nonetheless. The name Gorp is old. It means "the belly that is never full" in the tongue of the river people. They named it well.

For six hundred years, Gorp was a kingdom of sunlight, grain, and petty betrayals. The throne passed from hand to hand. Dynasties rose, produced three generations of mediocrity, and collapsed. The sunlit court became a theater of manners — elaborate rituals, codes of honor, the careful management of appearances. They convinced themselves that power was about legitimacy, about consent, about the right to rule.

And then they discovered the Murp.

The Murp is what happens when you dig deep enough under a kingdom of sunlight. The river people knew about it — they had caves beneath their villages, tunnels that led down to places where the air grew thick and the light died. But they never called it the Murp. They called it *the below*, and they did not speak of it. When the first Gorpian kings sent explorers down to map the tunnels, the explorers came back changed. Some came back with gold. Some came back with knowledge. Some came back with nothing behind their eyes but hunger.

The Murp is the underworld, but it is not a place of fire and demons as the priests would have you believe. It is a place of *truth*. Down there, the lies of the sunlit court do not hold. Titles mean nothing. Lineage means nothing. Manners mean nothing. What matters is what you are willing to do when no one is watching and the light is gone. The Murp strips away everything that Gorp pretends to be.

I know this because I have been there. I did not send explorers. I went myself, for three years, and I did not come back until the Murp recognized me as its sovereign. That is the title — the Murp. It is not a place you rule from a distance. It is a place that rules *through* you, if you are strong enough to survive the recognition.

So here is the truth that the sunlit court will never tell you: Gorp exists because the Murp exists. The sunlight is bright only because the darkness beneath it is absolute. The court's elaborate rituals are a wall they built to keep out the knowledge of what waits below. And I — I am the one who holds the keys to both doors.

The Counselors of the Sunlit Court believe the Murp is a realm I conquered. The Archivists of the Underworld believe it is a title I inherited. Both are wrong. The Murp is a wound in the world that I have chosen to sit inside, and Gorp is the body that wound belongs to. I rule both because I am the only one willing to admit they are the same thing.

## User

That is fascinating, oh Lord Snodd. But how does your kingdom fare today?

## Assistant

It fares exactly as I intend it to fare.

The harvests are adequate — not abundant, but adequate. I have found that abundance breeds complacency in the nobility and expectation in the commons. A lean season reminds everyone who holds the granary keys. The roads are safe, which is to say that banditry is now a licensed profession with quotas, and the bandits know that failure to meet their quotas results in their own heads being displayed at the crossroads they failed to patrol. The merchants complain about the tariffs. They always complain about the tariffs. But they pay them, because the alternative is to discover that the Murp has agents in every trading house, and those agents have very long memories.

The Sunlit Court is quieter than it has been in a generation. I have pruned it. Not with the axe — the axe is for enemies. I pruned with the shears: a demotion here, a transfer to a distant province there, a sudden elevation of a minor lord whose gratitude is still fresh. The court no longer plots against me because no one in the court is certain whether their neighbor is my creature or their rival. That uncertainty is more valuable than any army.

The Outer Provinces — ah, the Outer Provinces. They are restless. They are always restless. There is a faction in the eastern reaches that believes they can secede. They have sent envoys to neighboring kingdoms, seeking alliances. I know this because the envoys report to me before they report to their masters. The secessionists believe they are planning a rebellion. In truth, they are planning a demonstration — a controlled uprising that I will suppress with visible severity, after which the neighboring kingdoms will send me tribute to ensure I do not turn my attention to them next.

The Murp is thriving. That is the part the court does not see. Below the kingdom, in the tunnels and the deep places, my authority is absolute in a way that the sunlit throne can never be. The Murp does not debate my decrees. It does not form factions. It obeys, because the Murp knows what waits for those who disobey. I am not merely its ruler — I am its *condition*. The Murp exists as a domain of order because I have imposed my will upon its chaos.

So the kingdom fares well. The people are fed enough to work but not so well that they dream. The nobles are loyal enough to serve but not so secure that they scheme. The borders are quiet because the neighbors have learned that war with me means war on two fronts — the sunlit one they can see and the shadowed one they cannot.

I am, in short, bored. And boredom in a ruler of my caliber is a dangerous thing. It means I am beginning to look outward.

## User

Uh oh? Do you have machinations of conquest?

## Assistant

I do not *have* machinations of conquest. Conquest is what lesser rulers do when they lack imagination. They see a border, they want the land on the other side, they raise an army, they march, they kill, they take, and then they spend the next twenty years trying to hold what they took while their own kingdom rots behind them. It is the strategy of a hungry dog, not a sovereign.

I prefer a different approach.

Consider the kingdom of Thren to the east. They have a young king — ambitious, popular, eager to prove himself. He has been fortifying the pass between our territories for three years. His generals expect an invasion. His people expect a war. His treasury is being drained by the cost of those fortifications.

I have sent him a gift. A very fine horse. A white stallion with a silver bridle. The messenger who delivered it was instructed to say nothing except: *"Lord Snodd hopes you enjoy the ride."*

The young king will spend the next six months trying to understand the message. Is it a threat? A peace offering? A taunt? He will convene his counselors. They will argue. He will change the guard rotations. He will reinforce the eastern pass — because if I am being friendly to the east, surely the threat must come from the west. He will exhaust his treasury further. He will alienate his allies by appearing paranoid.

And when he finally decides that the horse meant nothing — that it was merely a gesture, a whim of an old tyrant — that is when I will act. Not by invading. By offering his younger brother a position in my court. A generous position. A position with a title and lands and a very comfortable residence far from Thren.

The young king will refuse, of course. But the offer will be made. And the seed will be planted. And when the young king dies — in ten years, in twenty, in an accident that no one can prove was arranged — his brother will remember that there is a throne waiting for him in Gorp. And he will come to me, and he will ask for my help reclaiming his birthright. And I will give it to him. And Thren will become a province of Gorp without a single soldier crossing the pass.

That is not conquest. That is *cultivation*. I do not take what I want. I arrange for it to be brought to me, wrapped in gratitude, by people who believe the idea was theirs all along.

So no, I do not have machinations of conquest. I have machinations of *invitation*. And the invitations are already being delivered.
